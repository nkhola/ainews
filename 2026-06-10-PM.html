Macro Briefing: June 11, 2026 – Iran Escalation, AI Capex Bottlenecks, and the SpaceX Signal

1. The Strait of Hormuz Toggle

Oil surged after the US completed a second wave of strikes on Iran, with Tehran responding by halting all vessel traffic through the Strait of Hormuz. The fragile ceasefire is now clearly broken. The mechanism here is straightforward: 20% of global oil transit passes through that 21-mile chokepoint. Brent crude spiked, but the move was trimmed after the US declared the strikes "completed," suggesting a limited rather than escalatory intent. The real risk is a prolonged disruption, which would feed directly into inflation expectations and compress equity multiples.

Oil tankers are already "sneaking through" the strait, per analyst reports. That's a risk premium not yet fully priced into spreads. The strategic read: Trump's Iran war has paradoxically accelerated China's cleantech push, because disrupted energy supply chains make alternatives more attractive. US manufacturing reshoring remains stuck on skills shortages and tariff uncertainty, so the inflation impulse from higher oil will hit domestic consumption disproportionately.

Market impact: Energy sector gets a tactical bid, but the broader market is pricing this as a contained event. Watch crude volumes through tonight. If the strait remains closed past 48 hours, the volatility regime changes.

2. AI Infrastructure: The Capex Supercycle Meets a Material Constraint

Broadcom, Apollo Global Management, and Blackstone announced a $35 billion capital solution to fund 20+ gigawatts of AI compute capacity through 2028, including 1 GW for Anthropic. Apollo shares rose 5.5% on the news. This is not a marginal project; it is a signal that private credit has become the primary funding mechanism for AI infrastructure, bypassing traditional bank lending. The scale is staggering for a sector that is capital-heavy and still lacking clear monetization paths.

But the real story is the bottleneck. China's control over indium phosphide exports threatens AI data center rollout. Coherent's CEO flew to China with Trump's delegation to raise license delays. Indium phosphide is essential for photonics and high-speed data center optics. If this supply chain kink tightens, it delays the capacity that Broadcom and Apollo just pledged to build. That is a structural risk to the entire AI hardware thesis.

Valuation context: NVDA and AVGO trade at rich multiples that already assume 50%+ revenue growth from data center products for the next two years. Any delivery lag on the physical inputs would compress those multiples faster than a demand shock. Keep an eye on Coherent's earnings call transcripts for actual license status.

3. SpaceX IPO: The Elephant in the Room While Tech Stocks Sink

SpaceX's IPO is reported to be over 4 times oversubscribed, a massive demand signal for the most credible private space company. Meanwhile, US tech stocks are sinking on broader volatility. Oracle tumbled after earnings, continuing the pattern of enterprise software being punished for any hint of slowing cloud growth. The contrast is instructive: SpaceX is seen as a generational monopolist in launch, with Starlink as a cash flow engine. The public market is rationing capital for legacy tech while flooding into the one name that offers a new growth vector.

The rotation is not uniform. Megacap tech (AAPL, MSFT, GOOG, AMZN, META) is holding better than second-tier software. The risk is that SpaceX's debut absorbs liquidity and attention, pulling capital away from other high-multiple names. The IPO itself is a signal that the private market believes public valuations can support a $250B+ market cap, which sets a floor for space-adjacent plays but also raises the opportunity cost for holding anything else.

Mechanism: Four times oversubscribed means institutional allocation will be tiny, creating a scarcity premium on day one. Retail will chase. That dynamic is a sentiment indicator, not a fundamental one, but it tells you where the narrative tailwinds are strongest.

4. Activist Pressure and Guidance Drift

Elliott Management escalated its pressure on Northern Star Resources, urging a strategic review and sale options. In gold mining, activists see a consolidation opportunity after years of margin compression. Separately, Oxford Industries guided FY2026 EPS below consensus while narrowing its sales outlook. Stitch Fix guided revenue of $1.35B with positive adjusted EBITDA, essentially confirming that the turnaround is anaemic.

These are small data points, but they fit a pattern: corporate guidance is being ratcheted down across consumer-facing and commodity sectors, while the AI capex supercycle narrative dominates headlines. The divergence between what companies are saying and what the market is pricing (AI stocks at 30x+ sales, consumer stocks at 10x earnings) remains the widest it has been since 2021.
